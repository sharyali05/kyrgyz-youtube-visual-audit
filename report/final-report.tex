% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent Though online platforms claim to amplify Indigenous voices, Kyrgyz communities are worried that YouTube is instead eroding their language and culture. Prior algorithmic audit work has demonstrated that YouTube disproportionately recommends Russian-language content to Kyrgyz children, even when they signal preferences for Kyrgyz-language content. Where that work focused on \textit{which} content gets recommended, this study asks: \textit{what does that content look like}, and what do those visual differences signal ideologically? Drawing on ethnographic interviews and participant observation in Bishkek, Kyrgyzstan, we identify community perceptions that Russian-language children's content is more dynamic, colorful, and professionally produced than Kyrgyz-language content. We operationalize these perceptions as measurable visual features extracted from YouTube thumbnails — including luminance, color saturation, color composition, text overlay density, and CNN-derived embeddings — and test whether they hold up computationally across a labeled corpus of 800 YouTube videos (710 with recoverable thumbnails). We find that Russian-language thumbnails are significantly brighter than Kyrgyz-language thumbnails (Mann-Whitney U, $r = 0.36$, $p < 0.001$ after Bonferroni correction), and that Russian and English thumbnails show higher text overlay rates consistent with a commercial vlogger aesthetic (19.8\% and 20.8\% vs. 13.2\%, $r = 0.11$, $p = 0.065$). When restricting analysis to child-targeted content only, the luminance effect strengthens to a large effect size ($r = 0.55$, $p = 0.0002$), and a striking content scarcity finding emerges: only 12.1\% of Kyrgyz-labeled content targets children, compared to 29.4\% of Russian content. Unexpectedly, Kyrgyz thumbnails show higher blue pixel ratios than Russian ones (median 0.132 vs. 0.065), suggesting a \textit{distinct} rather than simply deficient visual register. A CNN embedding analysis using MobileNetV2 and UMAP reveals that while language groups overlap substantially in visual space (silhouette score = 0.078), the Kyrgyz centroid is displaced from Russian and English centroids by a Euclidean distance of 2.73. We discuss how these visual asymmetries reinforce language ideologies that associate Russian with modernity and Kyrgyz with tradition, and consider implications for platform design and Indigenous digital sovereignty.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} algorithmic auditing; language ideology; visual semiotics; YouTube; Kyrgyz; colonial amplification; digital media; thumbnails
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Thumbnail Dataset}

For the visual analysis, we extracted thumbnail images for up to 200 videos per language group (800 total). YouTube thumbnails were fetched programmatically using standard HTTPS requests, retrieving the highest available resolution for each video (\texttt{maxresdefault} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{hqdefault} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{mqdefault} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{default}). Videos that were deleted, private, or otherwise unavailable returned placeholder images, which were detected and excluded based on image height. Of the 800 videos targeted, 710 (88.8\%) yielded usable thumbnails, distributed as follows: 182 Kyrgyz, 178 other, 177 Russian, and 173 English.

% ── 4. Methods ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods [Shary]}

\subsection{Rationale for Thumbnail Analysis}

We use thumbnails rather than full video frames as our unit of analysis for three reasons. First, thumbnails are lightweight and fast to retrieve at scale, making them tractable for large-corpus analysis without the bandwidth and storage requirements of video download. Second, thumbnails are designed by content creators to be maximally visually representative of their content; they are, in effect, the visual face that a video presents to potential viewers, optimized for attention and click-through. Third, thumbnails are the primary visual signal that the YouTube recommendation interface presents to users browsing content, making them the most ecologically valid unit for studying the visual landscape of recommended content. All analysis notebooks were run in Google Colab with outputs persisted to Google Drive; code is available at the repository listed above.

\subsection{Color and Luminance Feature Extraction}

For each thumbnail, we computed the following features using the HSV (Hue-Saturation-Value) color space via OpenCV \citep{opencv}:

\begin{itemize}
\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\setlength{\parskip}{0pt}
    \item \textbf{Mean luminance}: Average value (V channel) across all pixels, ranging 0--255
    \item \textbf{Mean saturation}: Average saturation (S channel) across all pixels, ranging 0--255
    \item \textbf{Saturation and luminance variability}: Standard deviation of S and V channels respectively
    \item \textbf{Mean hue}: Average hue (H channel) across all pixels
    \item \textbf{Color ratios}: Proportion of pixels classified as red/orange (H $\leq$ 15 or H $\geq$ 160, S $>$ 50), blue (100 $\leq$ H $\leq$ 130, S $>$ 50), and yellow (20 $\leq$ H $\leq$ 35, S $>$ 80)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Text Overlay Detection}

To measure vlogger-style text overlays, we used a lightweight edge-density method rather than full OCR to avoid memory constraints. For each thumbnail, we computed: (1) \textbf{text cell ratio}: the proportion of an 8$\times$6 image grid whose cells exceed an edge density threshold of 0.15, as detected by Canny edge detection; (2) \textbf{contrast score}: overall image contrast via Otsu thresholding; and (3) a binary \textbf{text overlay proxy} flag when text cell ratio exceeded 0.30. This approach detects high-contrast, text-like regions without distinguishing text from other high-contrast visual elements, a limitation we address in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{CNN Embedding Analysis}

To assess the overall visual similarity structure of the corpus, we extracted 1280-dimensional feature vectors from each thumbnail using MobileNetV2 \citep{sandler2018}, a lightweight convolutional neural network pretrained on ImageNet \citep{deng2009}, with the classification head removed and global average pooling applied. These high-dimensional embeddings were reduced to two dimensions using UMAP \citep{mcinnes2018} with cosine distance metric, 15 nearest neighbors, and minimum distance of 0.1. Cluster separation was quantified using the silhouette score \citep{rousseeuw1987}.

\subsection{Demographic Subgroup Analysis}

Each video in the corpus was labeled with a most-recommended demographic category (child, mixed, or adult) by research assistants. To test whether visual differences between language groups hold specifically within child-targeted content — the most theoretically relevant comparison for a study of children's media — we conducted a secondary analysis restricted to videos labeled as child-targeted. Mann-Whitney U tests and rank-biserial effect sizes were computed for this subset using the same procedure as the full-corpus analysis, without Bonferroni correction given the exploratory and confirmatory nature of the subgroup analysis.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

All pairwise comparisons between language groups used the Mann-Whitney U test (two-sided), a non-parametric test appropriate for non-normally distributed visual features. Effect sizes were computed as rank-biserial correlations ($r$), interpreted as negligible ($r < 0.1$), small ($0.1 \leq r < 0.3$), medium ($0.3 \leq r < 0.5$), or large ($r \geq 0.5$) following \citet{cohen1988}. To control for multiple comparisons across seven features and three language pairs (21 total comparisons), we applied Bonferroni correction ($\alpha_{\text{corrected}} = 0.05 / 21 = 0.00238$). All analyses were conducted in Python using SciPy \citep{scipy}, pandas \citep{pandas}, and scikit-learn \citep{sklearn}.

% ── 5. Results ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Results [Shary]}

The core analysis compared 532 videos across three language groups (177 Russian, 182 Kyrgyz, 173 English) with successfully retrieved thumbnails. Seven visual features were tested. Kruskal-Wallis tests identified five features with significant overall group differences: mean luminance ($H = 60.95$, $p < 0.001$), mean saturation ($H = 20.57$, $p < 0.001$), red/orange ratio ($H = 7.26$, $p = 0.026$), blue ratio ($H = 7.12$, $p = 0.028$), and saturation variability ($H = 6.61$, $p = 0.037$). Post-hoc Bonferroni-corrected pairwise comparisons yielded three significant results, all involving luminance.

% ── 6. Discussion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion [Shary]}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Luminance and the Aesthetics of Prestige}

The finding that Russian-language thumbnails are significantly brighter than Kyrgyz ones directly corroborates what caregivers reported in ethnographic interviews. This brightness differential is not merely a matter of production quality; it reflects the visual grammar of commercial children's entertainment, in which high luminance signals energy, excitement, and attention-worthiness. The commercial children's entertainment industry has converged on high-brightness, high-saturation visual norms precisely because these properties are effective at attracting child viewers' attention \citep{labrecque2012}. Russian-language content, embedded in this commercial ecosystem, inherits these norms; Kyrgyz-language content, produced largely by NGOs and independent creators, does not.

\subsection{Content Scarcity and the Child Demographic Gap}

The demographic subgroup analysis reveals a finding that extends beyond visual aesthetics into platform ecology. Only 12.1\% of Kyrgyz-labeled content in this corpus targets children, compared to 29.4\% of Russian content. This is not simply a reflection of what Kyrgyz creators choose to produce — it reflects a structural condition in which the Kyrgyz children's media ecosystem on YouTube is thin, dominated by a small number of institutionally produced programs (notably \textit{Keremet Koch}, funded by UNICEF and the Aga Khan Foundation) rather than the diverse commercial children's entertainment that fills the Russian-language recommendation pool. The platform's recommendation algorithm, operating on engagement signals, naturally amplifies the content that performs best on those metrics, and a sparse, institutionally produced Kyrgyz children's library is structurally disadvantaged relative to Russia's large commercial children's entertainment ecosystem. The content scarcity finding thus reframes the visual difference: Kyrgyz children are not only being recommended foreign-language content, they are being recommended content produced under fundamentally different material conditions than the Kyrgyz children's content that exists on the platform.

\subsection{The Blue Ratio Reversal: Distinct, Not Deficient}

The elevated blue pixel ratio in Kyrgyz thumbnails is the most theoretically generative finding of this study. Rather than simply falling below Russian content on every visual metric, Kyrgyz thumbnails occupy a different aesthetic register, one characterized by cooler, bluer palettes. Several interpretations are worth considering. First, Kyrgyz cultural content frequently foregrounds outdoor, rural settings (mountains, sky, and steppe) which naturally produce higher blue ratios. Second, a significant portion of Kyrgyz-language children's content is produced by UNICEF and the Aga Khan Foundation, organizations whose branding conventions favor blue palettes. Third, and most theoretically significant, the blue ratio finding cautions against interpreting the visual gap between Russian and Kyrgyz content as a straightforward quality deficit. The language ideology literature cautions against framing heritage language content as merely inferior to dominant-language content \citep{woolard1998, irvine2000}. The blue ratio finding suggests that Kyrgyz-language YouTube content is not simply a lower-quality version of Russian content, but reflects different production contexts, funding sources, and aesthetic conventions.

\subsection{CNN Embeddings and Visual Structure}

The near-zero silhouette scores (0.020 on raw embeddings, 0.078 on UMAP coordinates) indicate substantial overlap between language groups in CNN visual space. This finding is complementary to, rather than contradictory with, the statistical differences found in specific metrics. The statistical analysis found that Russian thumbnails are significantly brighter on average ($r = 0.36$), but the CNN embedding analysis shows that this brightness difference does not translate into wholesale visual separation, the two groups share much of the same visual territory. This means the visual difference between Russian and Kyrgyz content is real but partial: concentrated in specific measurable dimensions rather than reflecting a total difference in visual character. This is consistent with the ``distinct register'' argument: the Kyrgyz centroid's displacement of 2.73 UMAP units from Russian (vs. only 0.80 units from English) suggests Kyrgyz content is pulled in a different visual direction, even if individual thumbnails from all groups intermingle.

% ── 7. Limitations ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations [Shary]}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
\setlength{\itemsep}{0pt}
\setlength{\parskip}{0pt}
    \item \textbf{Thumbnail as proxy}: Thumbnails are designed for maximum visual appeal and may not accurately represent in-video aesthetics. Future work should replicate this analysis using sampled video frames.
    \item \textbf{Video availability}: 88 of 800 targeted videos (11.0\%) were unavailable at the time of thumbnail collection, potentially introducing systematic bias if deleted videos differ visually from available ones.
    \item \textbf{Child-targeted sample size}: The demographic subgroup analysis is based on only 22 child-targeted Kyrgyz videos, limiting statistical power for detecting smaller effects within this subset.
    \item \textbf{Text overlay detection}: The OpenCV edge-density method is a proxy measure that cannot distinguish text from other high-contrast visual elements such as geometric patterns or sharp color boundaries. Full OCR on video frames would provide more precise measurement.
    \item \textbf{Animation vs. live action}: Color and luminance metrics may behave differently for animated vs. live-action content. The corpus likely contains both, and future work should analyze these subgenres separately.
    \item \textbf{CNN generalization}: MobileNetV2 was pretrained on ImageNet (everyday objects) and may not capture the most culturally relevant visual distinctions between these content types. A model fine-tuned on YouTube thumbnails might show stronger cluster separation.
    \item \textbf{Causal inference}: This study is observational. Visual differences between language groups do not establish that thumbnail aesthetics cause children's language preferences or shift.
    \item \textbf{Temporal scope}: The URL corpus was collected at a specific point in time. YouTube's recommendation algorithm and the available content landscape change continuously.
\end{itemize}

% ── 8. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion [Shary]}

This study demonstrates that the Russian-language YouTube content disproportionately recommended to Kyrgyz children differs visually from Kyrgyz-language content in ways that are measurable, theoretically meaningful, and consistent with community perceptions. Russian-language thumbnails are significantly brighter than Kyrgyz ones — a medium effect ($r = 0.36$) that strengthens to large ($r = 0.55$, $p = 0.0002$) when the analysis is restricted to child-targeted content. Russian and English thumbnails align more closely with the commercial vlogger aesthetic — higher text overlay, warmer color palettes, higher contrast — associated with international commercial children's media. These visual asymmetries do not merely reflect differences in production quality; they reflect differences in production context, funding, and the aesthetic conventions of commercial entertainment versus institutional or independent content creation.
