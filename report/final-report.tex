% ── Abstract ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
\noindent Though online platforms claim to amplify Indigenous voices, Kyrgyz communities are worried that YouTube is instead eroding their language and culture. Prior algorithmic audit work has demonstrated that YouTube disproportionately recommends Russian-language content to Kyrgyz children, even when they signal preferences for Kyrgyz-language content. Where that work focused on \textit{which} content gets recommended, this study asks: \textit{what does that content look like}, and what do those visual differences signal ideologically? Drawing on ethnographic interviews and participant observation in Bishkek, Kyrgyzstan, we identify community perceptions that Russian-language children's content is more dynamic, colorful, and professionally produced than Kyrgyz-language content. We operationalize these perceptions as measurable visual features extracted from YouTube thumbnails — including luminance, color saturation, color composition, text overlay density, and CNN-derived embeddings — and test whether they hold up computationally across a labeled corpus of 800 YouTube videos. We find that Russian-language thumbnails are significantly brighter than Kyrgyz-language thumbnails (Mann-Whitney U, $r = 0.36$, $p < 0.05$), and that Russian and English thumbnails show higher text overlay rates consistent with a commercial vlogger aesthetic. Unexpectedly, Kyrgyz thumbnails show higher blue pixel ratios, suggesting a \textit{distinct} rather than simply deficient visual register. A CNN embedding analysis using MobileNetV2 and UMAP reveals that while language groups overlap substantially in visual space, Kyrgyz content occupies a distinct spatial position from Russian and English content. We discuss how these visual asymmetries reinforce language ideologies that associate Russian with modernity and Kyrgyz with tradition, and consider implications for platform design and Indigenous digital sovereignty.

\medskip
\noindent \textbf{Keywords:} algorithmic auditing; language ideology; visual semiotics; YouTube; Kyrgyz; colonial amplification; digital media; thumbnails
\end{abstract}

\subsection{Thumbnail Dataset}

For the visual analysis, we extracted thumbnail images for up to 200 videos per language group. YouTube thumbnails were fetched programmatically using standard HTTPS requests, retrieving the highest available resolution for each video (\texttt{maxresdefault} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{hqdefault} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{mqdefault} $\rightarrow$ \texttt{default}). Videos that were deleted, private, or otherwise unavailable returned placeholder images, which were detected and excluded based on image height. Of the 800 videos targeted, 710 (88.8\%) yielded usable thumbnails.

% ── 4. Methods ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Methods}

\subsection{Rationale for Thumbnail Analysis}

We use thumbnails rather than full video frames as our unit of analysis for three reasons. First, thumbnails are lightweight and fast to retrieve at scale, making them tractable for large-corpus analysis without the bandwidth and storage requirements of video download. Second, thumbnails are designed by content creators to be maximally visually representative of their content — they are, in effect, the visual face that a video presents to potential viewers, optimized for attention and click-through. Third, thumbnails are the primary visual signal that the YouTube recommendation interface presents to users browsing content, making them the most ecologically valid unit for studying the visual landscape of recommended content.

\subsection{Color and Luminance Feature Extraction}

For each thumbnail, we computed the following features using the HSV (Hue-Saturation-Value) color space via OpenCV \citep{opencv}:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Mean luminance}: Average value (V channel) across all pixels, ranging 0--255
    \item \textbf{Mean saturation}: Average saturation (S channel) across all pixels, ranging 0--255
    \item \textbf{Saturation and luminance variability}: Standard deviation of S and V channels
    \item \textbf{Mean hue}: Average hue (H channel) across all pixels
    \item \textbf{Color ratios}: Proportion of pixels classified as red/orange (H $\leq$ 15 or H $\geq$ 160, S $>$ 50), blue (100 $\leq$ H $\leq$ 130, S $>$ 50), and yellow (20 $\leq$ H $\leq$ 35, S $>$ 80)
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Text Overlay Detection}

To measure vlogger-style text overlays, we used a lightweight edge-density method rather than full OCR to avoid memory constraints. For each thumbnail, we computed: (1) \textbf{text cell ratio} — the proportion of an 8$\times$6 image grid whose cells exceed an edge density threshold of 0.15, as detected by Canny edge detection; (2) \textbf{contrast score} — overall image contrast via Otsu thresholding; and (3) a binary \textbf{text overlay proxy} flag when text cell ratio exceeded 0.30. This approach detects high-contrast, text-like regions without distinguishing text from other high-contrast visual elements — a limitation we address in Section~\ref{sec:limitations}.

\subsection{CNN Embedding Analysis}

To assess the overall visual similarity structure of the corpus, we extracted 1280-dimensional feature vectors from each thumbnail using MobileNetV2 \citep{sandler2018}, a lightweight convolutional neural network pretrained on ImageNet \citep{deng2009}, with the classification head removed and global average pooling applied. These high-dimensional embeddings were reduced to two dimensions using UMAP \citep{mcinnes2018} with cosine distance metric, 15 nearest neighbors, and minimum distance of 0.1. Cluster separation was quantified using the silhouette score \citep{rousseeuw1987}.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

All pairwise comparisons between language groups used the Mann-Whitney U test (two-sided), a non-parametric test appropriate for non-normally distributed visual features. Effect sizes were computed as rank-biserial correlations ($r$), interpreted as negligible ($r < 0.1$), small ($0.1 \leq r < 0.3$), medium ($0.3 \leq r < 0.5$), or large ($r \geq 0.5$) following \citet{cohen1988}. To control for multiple comparisons across seven features and three language pairs, we applied Bonferroni correction ($\alpha = 0.05 / 21 = 0.0024$). All analyses were conducted in Python using SciPy \citep{scipy}, pandas \citep{pandas}, and scikit-learn \citep{sklearn}.

Russian-language thumbnails are significantly brighter than Kyrgyz-language thumbnails (Mann-Whitney U, $r = 0.36$, $p < 0.05$ after Bonferroni correction), representing a medium effect size. The median luminance of Russian thumbnails (156.7) exceeds that of Kyrgyz thumbnails (127.3) by approximately 23\% on the 0--255 scale. \subsection{CNN Embedding Analysis}

The silhouette score on raw MobileNetV2 embeddings was 0.020, and on 2D UMAP coordinates was 0.078, both below the 0.2 threshold for meaningful cluster separation. This indicates that Russian, Kyrgyz, and English thumbnails do not occupy clearly distinct territories in CNN visual space. However, the centroid positions reveal a meaningful spatial pattern: the Kyrgyz centroid ($x = -2.53$, $y = 1.64$) is substantially displaced from both Russian ($x = 0.18$, $y = 1.27$) and English ($x = 0.97$, $y = 1.01$) centroids, with a Euclidean distance of 2.73 between Russian and Kyrgyz centroids. % ── 7. Limitations ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Thumbnail as proxy}: Thumbnails are designed for maximum visual appeal and may not accurately represent in-video aesthetics. Future work should replicate this analysis using sampled video frames.
    \item \textbf{Video availability}: Many videos in the corpus were unavailable at the time of thumbnail collection, reducing effective sample sizes and potentially introducing systematic bias if deleted videos differ visually from available ones.
    \item \textbf{Text overlay detection}: The OpenCV edge-density method cannot distinguish text from other high-contrast visual elements. Full OCR on video frames would provide more precise measurement.
    \item \textbf{Animation vs. live action}: Color and luminance metrics may behave differently for animated vs. live-action content. Future work should analyze these subgenres separately.
    \item \textbf{CNN generalization}: MobileNetV2 was pretrained on ImageNet (everyday objects) and may not capture the most culturally relevant visual distinctions between these content types. A model fine-tuned on YouTube thumbnails might show stronger separation.
    \item \textbf{Causal inference}: This study is observational. Visual differences between language groups do not establish that thumbnail aesthetics cause children's language preferences or shift.
    \item \textbf{Temporal scope}: The URL corpus was collected at a specific point in time. YouTube's recommendation algorithm and available content change continuously.
\end{itemize}
